\qa{What is the primary physical limitation of the pure pinhole camera model, and why do real cameras introduce a physical lens?}{
A pure pinhole camera forces a severe physical trade-off between \textbf{image sharpness and light intensity}. A tiny pinhole creates a perfectly sharp image but lets in almost no light, while a larger pinhole lets in enough light but severely blurs the image.

Introducing a lens solves this by allowing the camera to capture a massive amount of light (large aperture) while actively bending the rays to converge and maintain sharp focus.

Image blur caused by light wave diffraction is dictated by the \textbf{aperture size} (smaller apertures increase diffraction spread), not by the physical thickness of the lens itself. To minimize diffraction, you must use a wider aperture (lower f-number).}

\qa{In the thin lens model, what mathematical condition determines whether an object is in focus, and what geometric phenomenon creates a "circle of confusion"?}{
\begin{itemize}
    \item Focus is strictly governed by the \textbf{thin lens equation}:
    \begin{center}
        $ \displaystyle \frac{1}{d_0} + \frac{1}{d_1} = \frac{1}{f} $
    \end{center}
    where $d_0$ is object distance, $d_1$ is image (sensor) distance, and $f$ is the focal length.
    \item Only light rays originating from the exact spatial distance $d_0$ will converge perfectly to a single point on the sensor. 
    \item Light rays from objects at any other distance will converge either in front of or behind the sensor. When these unconverged rays hit the sensor plane, they form a blurred, circular patch of light known as a \textbf{circle of confusion}. Introducing a physical barrier (stopping down the aperture) artificially narrows the light cone, reducing the diameter of this circle and expanding the depth of field.
\end{itemize}}

\qa{How is radial distortion mathematically modeled and corrected in standard computer vision pipelines (like OpenCV)?}{
Because the severity of radial distortion is a non-linear function of the radial distance $r$ from the image center, it is analytically approximated using a Taylor series polynomial.

The corrected, ideal pixel coordinates $(x_{corr}, y_{corr})$ are computed from the raw, distorted coordinates $(x, y)$ using derived distortion coefficients ($k_1, k_2, k_3$): 
\begin{center}
    $ \displaystyle x_{corr} = x \cdot (1 + k_1 r^2 + k_2 r^4 + k_3 r^6) $ \\[1ex]
    $ \displaystyle y_{corr} = y \cdot (1 + k_1 r^2 + k_2 r^4 + k_3 r^6) $
\end{center}
}

\qa{What is the physical optical mechanism behind chromatic aberration, and how does it manifest in the final image?}{
Chromatic aberration is caused by \textbf{optical dispersion}. The refractive index of the glass lens is not a constant; it depends on the wavelength of the incoming light. 

As a result, the focal length $f(\lambda)$ varies slightly for different colors (e.g., blue light bends at a steeper angle than red light). This failure to focus all color wavelengths at the exact same convergence point results in noticeable \textbf{color fringes} (unnatural purple or green halos) along high-contrast structural edges, particularly near the outer boundaries of the image.}

\qa{When performing inverse projection (mapping a 2D pixel back to a 3D physical ray) with a non-ideal camera, how must the computational pipeline be sequenced?}{
\begin{itemize}
    \item It is a fundamental error to blindly apply the pinhole inverse projection matrix to raw, distorted image pixels. The pipeline must be sequenced in strict reverse:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item \textbf{Compensate for Distortion:} First, apply the polynomial mathematical correction to map the raw, distorted pixel coordinate to its ideal, undistorted coordinate on the virtual image plane.
        \item \textbf{Apply Inverse Pinhole Matrix:} Only after the point is computationally undistorted can you legitimately apply the idealized thin lens / pinhole camera model to trace the 3D ray back into the real world.
    \end{enumerate}
\end{itemize}}
