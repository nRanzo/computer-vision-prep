Low vs mid vs high image processing / In a CV systems pipeline, how do low-level tasks compare versus mid-level tasks?

Low: single pixel and histogram-based transform, linear filters, non-linear filters
Mid: edge detection, derivate, corner detection, blob detection, hough transform, segmentation, feature detection (Keypoints detection and feature descriptor calculation, feature matching)
High: object detection and recognition, image (semantic) segmentation, ML for CV, projective geometry, image formation and cameras, camera calibration, DL for CV

Low-level processing focuses on image filtering, gray-level/color processing, and image enhancement (e.g., single-pixel transforms, DFT, local operators).
Mid-level processing is based on extracting more complex models from the image, such as line detection, blob detection, salient features/keypoints, and edge detection algorithms.

Define point operations and explain the difference between Logarithmic and Power-Law (Gamma) transformations

Point ops are spatial domain image processing techniques where the new value of a pixel depends strictly on the intesity value of that same pixel in the original image, without considering its neightbors. This is expressed as s = T(s), where r is the original intesity, s is the new intensity, and T is the transformation function.

logarithmic transform s = c log(1 + r) expands the values of dark pixels while compressing higher-level values. It is primarly used to compress the dynamic range of an image with huge variations in pixel values, such as displaying the FT spectrum.
Power-law / gamma tr. s = cr^gamma is more versatile than the log tr. as by altering the gamma value, it can both expand dark regions (when gamma < 1) or expand bright regions (when gamma > 1). It is most commonly used for gamma correction to adjust an image's contrast to match the display specs.


4. Compare and contrast "Contrast Stretching" and "Thresholding ".

Two techniques that both rely on piecewise-linear transformation functions to manipulate image contrast, but they serve different goals.

contrast stretching:  expands a narrow range of intesity levels in an image to span the full available dynamic range. It is used to correct low-contrast images by making the darks darker and brights brighter, while preserving the relative order of pixel intesities. It is represented as three segments, in which tipically the one in middle has a slope coeff > 1 resulting in a img enhancement while the other two have slope coeff < 1 resulting in a img compression.
Thresholding: extreme form or constrast stretching used for image segmentation. It maps all pixel intensities below a specified threshold value to a single dark value (like 0) and all intesities above the threshold to a single bright value (like L-1). Unlike stretching, thresholding irreversibly destroys the original intesity variations, resulting in a binary image.


5. What is the main limitation of Histogram Equalization, and why might we use Histogram Specification (Matching) instead?

The primary limitation of Histogram Equalization is that it is a fully automatic, global operation. It aggressively forces the image's histogram to be as flat as possible. While this generally increases contrast, it cannot be controlled. In images with uniform backgrounds or mostly dark/light areas, equalization can artificially enhance background noise or wash out important details.

Histogram Specification / Matching solves this by giving the user control. Instead of forcing a flat histogram, it transforms the original image so that its histogram matches a specific, user-defined target histogram highlighting desired intesity ranges. This is useful for standardizing images from different sensors or preventing the over-enhancement caused by std equalization.

8. Explain the principle of the median filter and its primary advantage over linear smoothing filters.

The median filter is a non-linear spatial filter that replaces the center pixel of a neighborhood with the median intensity value of the pixel within that window. Its primarly advantage over linear filters (like the mean filter) is its superior ability to remove impulse noise (salt & pepper) without blurring the image excessively. While a mean filter averages the extreme noise values into the surrounding pixels, creating a blur, the median filter discards the extreme outliers, preserving sharp edges and overall structure of the image.


9. How does the Bilateral Filter achieve edge-preserving smoothing, and how does it differ from a std Gaussian filter?

A std Gaussian filter smooths an image by computing a weighted avg of neighboring pixels based solely on their spatial distance from the center. This reduces noise but blurs edges. The Bilateral Filter is a non-lineral, edge-preserving technique that solves this by combining two Gaussian kernels: a spatial (domain) kernel and a range (intensity) kernel.
The spatial kernel weights pixels based on physical proximity, while the range kernel weights them based on intesity similarity. Consequently, the bilateral filter only averages pixel that are both physically close and similar in brightness, ensuring flat regions are smoothed and preserved sharp intensity transitions / edges.


10. In the context of 2D FT, how do low and high frequency translate to the visual components of an image?

When an img is transformed into the frequency domain, its spacial intesity variations are represented as sinusoidal waves of varying frequencies. Low freq. correspond to areas of the image where pixel intesities change slowly / are constant, i.e. smooth gradients, uniform backgound / illumination. They contain the vast majority of the image's energy and appearence. High freq. correspond to areas of rapid intesity transitions i.e. edges, fine details, random noise. Removing high freq. results in blurring, while emphasizing them sharpens the img.


11. Compare ideal LPF and Butterworth LPF (BLPF) in the frequency domain. Why is BLPF generally preferred in practise?

Both filters are used in the freq. domain to attenuate high frequencies and smooth an img, but they differ in their cutoff characteristics. The ideal LPF has an infinitely sharp cutoff radious  passing all frequencies below the threshold perfectly and cutting off all the higher freq. entirely. This truncation causes severe ringing artifacts (concentric ripples) in the resulting spatial img.
The BLPF introduces a smooth transition between the passed and attenuated freq., controlled by the filter's order . Because it avoids a sudden cutoff, it significantly minimizes / completely eliminate the ringing effect --> handy for img restoration.


12. How are geometric spatial transformations mathematically represented and applied to an image?

Geometric transformations modify the spatial relationship between pixels (e.g. translating, rotating, scaling, shearing … them). They are tipically represented using affine transformations via transformation matrices and homogeneous coordinates. It allows us to combine multiple sequential operations, such as scaling an image and then rotating it, into a single composite tranformation matrix. This matrix is then multiplied by the original pixel coordinates (x,y) to compute the new spatial locations.

14. How is a point represented in a std 2D coordinates vs homogeneous coordinates? And what is the structural advantage of using homogeneous coordinates vs std cartesian ones when performing geometric translations?

In a std 2D coordinates a point is represented by two values (x, y). In homogeneous coordinates an N-dimentional point is transformed into N + 1 dimensions using a mathematical trick, which is to map a (x, y) point into a three coordinate one (x~, y~, w~) that can eventually be conferted back to std coordinated by dividing it by the scaling factor w~ so to get x = x~/w~ and y = y~/w~.
In std coordinates, an affine transformation requires separate linear operations and additions (e.g. multiplying by matrix A and adding translation vector b). By switching to homogeneous coordinates, multiple ops (inlcuding translation) can be easily combined into a single matrix multiplication.


15. What makes an affine transform different from a basic linear transform, and what specific properties does it preserve?

An Affine transform is a more generic planar transformation that consists of a linear transform followed by a translation. While it moves and scales points, an Affine transform specifically preserves point collinearity (points on a line stay on a line) and distance ratios along a line.


16. When analyzing an image for edges, what is the conceptual difference between using a derivative filter versus a true edge detection algorithm? 

Derivative filters are a low-level operation; they simply highlight pixels where intensity changes rapidly. The resulting filtered image still lacks the actual concept of what an edge is.
Edge detection algorithms, on the other hand, belong to mid-level processing because they mathematically embed the concept or "model" of an edge to actively detect and classify those structural features.


Q3: How do first-order and second-order derivatives differ in their approach to edge detection?
Exam-Ready Answer: "Derivatives are used to detect rapid spatial changes in pixel intensity, which define edges.
First-order derivatives (like the Sobel or Prewitt operators) compute the gradient of the image. An edge is identified by locating the local maxima (the highest peaks) in the gradient magnitude. The gradient also provides the direction of the edge.
Second-order derivatives (like the Laplacian) compute the rate of change of the gradient. An edge is identified by locating zero-crossings—the exact point where the second derivative passes through zero. While second-order derivatives are better at pinpointing the exact center of an edge, they are exponentially more sensitive to high-frequency noise."
